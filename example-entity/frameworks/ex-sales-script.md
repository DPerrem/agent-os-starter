# Acme Co — Sales Discovery Script

> ⚠️ WORKED EXAMPLE. Use as the shape for your real sales/script frameworks.
>
> This is a 30-min discovery call template. Run before quoting any engagement.

---

## Purpose

Validate fit before quoting. Three things we're checking:

1. **Pain** — is there a real, urgent operational problem they care about solving?
2. **Decision** — are we talking to someone who can sign off on $30k+?
3. **Timing** — are they ready to engage in the next 60 days?

If any of these is no, we don't quote. We either qualify out or stay in touch.

---

## Pre-Call Prep (5 mins)

- Read their website. Note: industry, size, how long they've been around, anything quirky.
- Check LinkedIn for the person we're meeting. Tenure, prior roles, education.
- Pull any prior contact notes from CRM.

---

## Opening (3 mins)

> "Thanks for the time. Quick agenda — I'd like to spend 20 mins understanding what's going on operationally, then 5-10 mins on whether we're a fit and what next steps would look like. Sound good?"

Then: "Tell me a bit about [Company] in your words — what you do, what scale you're at, who your customers are."

(Listen. Don't pitch yet. Take notes.)

---

## Discovery (15-20 mins)

Ask in this order. Use their language back to them.

### 1. The trigger

> "What made you reach out now? What's prompting the conversation?"

→ If "we've been thinking about it" — soft trigger. Probably not urgent.
→ If "we just had X happen" or "we lost a customer because Y" — hard trigger. Worth pursuing.

### 2. The current state

> "Walk me through how [the relevant process] works today. Who's involved, what tools do you use, what's working, what's not."

→ Take detailed notes. Look for: spreadsheets being used as databases, manual handoffs between teams, things "we used to do but stopped."

### 3. The cost of inaction

> "If nothing changes in the next 12 months, what does that look like for you?"

→ This question separates real pain from theoretical pain. If they can't answer, the problem isn't urgent.

### 4. Prior attempts

> "Have you tried to fix this before? What happened?"

→ Critical. Tells us what won't work, what the political landscape is, who'll resist.

### 5. The decision

> "If we were to do something here, who would be involved in the decision? What does the budget conversation look like?"

→ If they're not the buyer, we need to know who is and how to get there.

### 6. Timing

> "What does the next 30-60 days look like for you? Are you in a position to start something, or is this more 'I'm gathering info'?"

→ Real timing question. We'd rather hear "not for 6 months" than "yes" that turns into a no.

---

## Close (5 mins)

If it's a fit:

> "Based on what you've shared, this sounds like a fit for our [Operations Modernisation Sprint]. Here's roughly how it works [60-second overview]. Next step would be a 90-min site visit, no charge, where I'd meet a couple of your team and get a fuller picture. After that, I'd send a proposal. Want to lock that in?"

If it's not a fit:

> "Honestly, I don't think we're the right fit for this — [specific reason]. What I'd suggest is [direction]. If things change in 6 months, give me a shout."

If unsure:

> "I want to think through whether we're the right fit. Can I come back to you in 48 hours with a recommendation either way?"

(Then actually do it. Don't ghost.)

---

## After the Call

- Within 24 hours: send a 4-line summary email — "here's what I heard, here's what I propose next, here's when I'll get back to you."
- Update CRM: stage, fit score, decision criteria, timing.
- File call notes in the deal folder.

---

## Don'ts

- Don't pitch before discovery. They'll tell you what they need; you don't need to guess.
- Don't quote on the call. Always come back with a written proposal.
- Don't promise outcomes you can't control. "We'll help you cut admin time" — fine. "We'll cut your admin time by 40%" — only if you have evidence.
- Don't trash competitors. If they mention one, ask "what did you like about them?" and "what didn't work?"
