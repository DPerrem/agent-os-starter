## Principles

The handful of ideas worth keeping. Each gets a name, a one-paragraph explanation, and a "where it applies" note.

### 1. Two-way doors vs one-way doors

**Idea:** Most decisions are two-way doors — if you walk through and don't like it, you can walk back. A few are one-way doors — once through, you can't return. Treat them differently. Don't run committees on two-way doors. Don't move fast on one-way doors.

**Where it applies:**
- Hiring (mostly two-way until past 90 days, then increasingly one-way).
- Pricing changes (two-way for new customers, one-way for existing).
- Product positioning (one-way once external messaging is out).

**Question to ask:** "If this turns out to be wrong, how hard is it to undo?"

### 2. Pre-mortems

**Idea:** Before committing to a plan, imagine it has failed catastrophically a year from now. Write the obituary. What killed it? You see weaknesses you missed when looking forward.

**Where it applies:**
- Any project with >$10k or >1 month committed.
- Any major hire.
- Any new offer launch.

**How to do it:** 30 minutes, alone, write the failure post-mortem in detail. Then look at the top 3 causes and decide whether they're acceptable risks or things to mitigate.

### 3. The cost of inaction

**Idea:** Decision frameworks usually overweight the cost of doing the new thing and underweight the cost of staying the same. Force yourself to articulate "what if we do nothing for 12 months?"

**Where it applies:** Any moment of analysis paralysis.
